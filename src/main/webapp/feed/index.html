We all like to take care of our skin: after all, it’s the largest single organ in the human body and the one which suffers the most from exposure to external wear and tear. Every day we put our skin at risk of damage from accidental injury, allergic reactions, sun exposure and pollution – not to mention the natural ageing process. But there are other, more insidious risks of skin damage that we may not recognize.

One of these is the ageing effect of smoking. It is many years now since smoking ceased to be regarded as ‘cool’ and ‘hip’ and in any way good for you, and much emphasis is placed on the recognized correlations between smoking and cancer. It’s a terrifying fact that 30% of all cancer deaths in developed countries can be attributed to smoking, and it accounts for more than 1 million annual deaths worldwide. [1]

Smoking and Skin Ageing

One of the not-so-high-profile risks is the correlation between smoking and skin ageing. Researchers have proved that smokers look older than non-smokers, because there is a direct connection between smoking and the reduced amounts of collagen in the skin. Investigation into the collagen-degrading enzyme matrix metalloproteinase 1 (MMP-1) shows that smokers have significantly more of this enzyme in their skin, meaning that they have less collagen in the skin than non-smokers. [2] Consumption of 20 cigarettes a day will age the appearance of your skin by nearly 10 years compared to than that of a non-smoker. [3]

Clearly, not smoking is the first step to preserving your skin, but you can also take measures to protect this most vulnerable of organs from external damage. Natural skincare products can help form a barrier between your sensitive epidermis and the various contaminants in the air, and will help your skin retain its moisture and elasticity.

So what exactly happens to your skin when you smoke? Exhaled smoke, whether it’s your own or it’s coming from others, is definitely detrimental [4]. The most damage is caused by the drying out of the skin’s surface, thus reducing the capacity of the capillaries to carry blood, and depriving your tissues of sufficient oxygen and the other nutrients they need to remain healthy. [4] Combined with the MMP-1 reducing the collagen, this produces premature wrinkles, which are particularly increased around the mouth and eyes, to the extent that smokers exhibit three times the amount of wrinkling compared to non-smokers. This effect increases after ten years’ worth of smoking (at the rate of 20 a day) or even five years’ worth (at 40 a day).[5]

Research has therefore proved that between 10 and 20 years of premature ageing can result from smoking, in addition to its serious effects on our internal organs. The whole appearance of our bodies can be affected by smoking, including our overall weight and shape as well as our skin. [4]

Moisturisers and barrier creams help to protect our sensitive skin, while anti-ageing products can aid in keeping the wrinkles at bay, but most important of all is to avoid smoking!
