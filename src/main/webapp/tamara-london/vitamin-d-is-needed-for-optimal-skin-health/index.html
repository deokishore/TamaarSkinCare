Healthy Skin Needs Vitamin D: Are we getting enough?

By Team TamaarMarch 23, 2016 Tamaar News
  • No Comments
  • 0
  • 0
    0

Why do we need vitamin D for a healthy, younger-looking skin? Modern research has demonstrated that what we call vitamin D is a far more complex combination of biological responses.

Technically, it is not even a vitamin, but rather something called a prohormone, produced in the skin by the photochemical conversion of ultraviolet light (specifically UVB). This prohormone eventually recombines through a series of chemical conversions to generate the vitamin D3, [1] which is essential to our physical well-being. In particular, it plays a vital role in the renewal and differentiation of our skin cells [2]. The keratinocytes, which are the predominant cell type in the outer most layer of the skin (or often referred to as basal epidermal cells) contain the biological machinery within themselves to metabolise vitamin D3, generating new daughter cells to replace the dead outer skin layer. [3]

More research is now coming through focusing on the direct relationship between vitamin D levels and skin aging. The capacity of the skin to produce the vitamin D3 is reduced by as much as half in older people, [11] and higher intake levels are therefore necessary.

In short, vitamin D helps in skin cell development and repair, which is needed for your skin to look fresh and healthy. Combined with good skincare, your skin will look better for longer.

We have all heard Vitamin D deficiency being linked with diseases, such as osteoporosis, some cancers and cardiovascular disease, [6] and also linked to autoimmune diseases such as Multiple Sclerosis, Type 1 diabetes and Rheumatoid arthritis. [7}. Studies also outline the role of the vitamin in the prevention of known skin diseases [8] and associate its deficiency with atopic dermatitis (eczema) [9] and teenage acne [10], therefore maintaining the optimal level of vitamin D is crucial. But are we getting enough?

Vitamin D Deficiencies

It is a shocking fact that more than 50% of the adult UK population has insufficient levels of Vitamin D. [5] The NHS recommended minimum daily intake for maintenance of a healthy population, depending on age, gender and physiological status, is 5µg (200IU). [12] In Contrast, Norway has set this minimum to 20µg (800IU) and Italy to 25 µg (1000IU).

We can generate sufficient levels of Vitamin D3 in our own skin by exposing it to as little as 10-15 minutes of sunlight a day, depending on various factors including geographical location, skin pigmentation and time of year.[13,14]

These days, we know that we must also be careful not to overdo it, particularly if we fall into one of the recognised high-risk groups, such as those of us with a fair skin, a lot of moles, or a family history of skin cancer. [15] We can boost our system instead with sources of Vitamin D, found mainly in oily fish, as well as in meat and eggs, or in fortified foods like breakfast cereals and some dairy products. A study published in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism, has also shown that Intake of probiotics may boost circulating Vitamin D levels in the blood by 25%. Generally, dietary sources alone are not enough to achieve optimal Vitamin D levels, and supplementation is necessary.

Vitamin D is crucial to the renewal of cells, so it keeps our skin looking healthy, while healthy skin in its turn makes more Vitamin D, so it’s a win-win situation!
